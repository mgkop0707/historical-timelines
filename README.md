# History Timelines

Interactive reference timelines on colonialism, treaty-making, and enslavement in the Americas. Built to help visualize complex, overlapping historical processes that are difficult to follow from reading or listening alone.

## Timelines

### 1. Plains Native American Treaties & Western Settlement Programs
**Period covered:** 1820–1892

A chronological record of US government treaties with six Plains nations — Lakota/Sioux, Cheyenne, Comanche, Arapaho, Crow, and Kiowa — plotted alongside their systematic abrogation and the concurrent federal land programs that transferred treaty lands to immigrant settlers.

Topics covered:
- Treaties signed with each nation and what they promised
- How and when each treaty was violated or nullified
- Federal land giveaway programs (Homestead Act, Dawes Act, etc.) that were explicitly promoted to relieve overcrowding in Eastern immigrant cities
- The legal and economic connection between treaty violations and immigrant settlement

### 2. Slavery in the Americas: A Comparative Timeline
**Period covered:** c. 650–1900 CE

A four-part comparative timeline of enslavement across European colonial powers, broken out by enslaved population. Deliberately includes Old World context to situate the Atlantic trade within a longer global history.

Sections:
- **Part I — Old World context:** Arab-run trans-Saharan trade, intra-African slavery, medieval European enslavement of Slavic peoples (the origin of the word "slave"), and Barbary Coast enslavement of Europeans
- **Part II — Americas by colonial power:** Portugal, Spain, Britain, France, the Netherlands, and the United States — each broken out by enslaved group (African, Native, Jewish/converso, European)
- **Part III — Transitions:** How one enslaved population succeeded another as demographics and economics shifted; the transition from legal slavery to convict leasing and debt peonage after abolition
- **Part IV — Scale:** Comparative numbers across all systems

Inspired by Gerald Horne, *The Dawning of the Apocalypse* (2020).

## How to use

Each timeline opens as a standalone webpage. All content is embedded — no internet connection required after the page loads.

- **Hover** over any dot or bar for a detailed description
- **Filter buttons** let you focus on a single nation or topic
- **Tabs** in the slavery timeline switch between the four sections
