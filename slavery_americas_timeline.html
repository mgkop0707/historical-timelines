Includes Old World context for intra-European enslavement of Slavic peoples, Arab-run trans-Saharan trade, and intra-African enslavement that preceded and fed the Atlantic trade. Inspired by Gerald Horne, The Dawning of the Apocalypse (2020).

Part I — Old World context: intra-European & pre-Atlantic African slavery (c. 650–1830)

Slavery was not invented by Atlantic colonialism. The word "slave" derives from "Slav" — Slavic peoples were among the most trafficked groups in medieval Europe and the Middle East. Arab-run trans-Saharan and Indian Ocean trades moved more Africans over a longer period than the Atlantic trade. These contexts shaped who was enslaved, how, and by whom when European colonizers built the Atlantic system. Hover bars for details.

Part III — Transitions: how enslaved populations succeeded one another

A recurring pattern in colonial slavery was the substitution of one enslaved population for another as demographics, economics, or politics shifted. The transition from legal slavery to quasi-slavery after formal abolition is particularly significant.
