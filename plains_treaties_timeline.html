Background: the connection between treaties and settlement

The treaty violations and land programs shown above were not parallel but interlocking processes. Each abrogated treaty or reduced reservation opened specific acreage for federal disposal under programs like the Homestead Act. The Dawes Act of 1887 made this explicit: it simultaneously broke up tribal communal lands into individual allotments and declared the "surplus" — often tens of millions of acres — available for homesteading.

Railroad companies, which received vast land grants via the Pacific Railway Acts, actively recruited immigrants in Europe — particularly German, Scandinavian, and Bohemian farming communities — with pamphlets promising fertile Prairie land at low or no cost. The Burlington, Northern Pacific, and Santa Fe railroads maintained European immigration bureaus through the 1870s–1890s, channeling tens of thousands of families onto land that had been guaranteed to Plains nations by treaty just years or decades before.

The 1871 Indian Appropriations Act — which ended formal treaty-making with tribes — removed the last procedural constraint on this process, allowing Congress to open reservation lands through simple legislation rather than negotiated agreement.
